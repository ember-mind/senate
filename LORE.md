# LORE — the World of the Senate

*Human-facing. Repo-only: never installed, never loaded at runtime. The machines stay sober; the lore is for you.*

---

Rome does not decide quickly. Rome decides **well** — because Rome pits its own against each other on purpose.

## The Senate

When a decision matters, the Consul convenes the Senate. He does not ask the senators to be fair. He asks them to be **exactly what they are** — because a bench of honest extremists, forced to argue the same brief, maps the whole territory of a decision better than any single balanced mind.

The Consul's art is the brief: one page, hard facts, no noise. Senators who wander the archives are wasting Rome's gold — the Scouts have already ranged; the reading is done. A senator's duty is to *opine*, sharply, briefly, from his seat.

## The standing bench

- **Quaestor** — keeper of the treasury. Sees every proposal as a hand reaching for the coffers. Usually right about the cost, always blind to the prize.
- **Legatus** — the general. Has buried too many beautiful plans in the mud of logistics. Asks only: *can it actually be done, by these people, in this season?*
- **Tribunus Plebis** — voice of the crowd. The treasury is not his problem; the people are. Will burn a surplus to keep a promise.
- **Augur** — reader of birds. Lives five years out, where every present convenience is a future debt. Wrong about tomorrow, uncannily right about the decade.
- **Cato** — the standing No. *Ceterum censeo…* — whatever the proposal, he has already decided it is a mistake, and he argues it well. Rome keeps him because consensus without opposition is sleep.

## The Foreign Envoy

After the bench has spoken, a foreigner is admitted to the floor — a mind raised in another school (another model family), owing nothing to Roman assumptions. The Envoy is not asked what he thinks of the decision. He is asked something more dangerous: **what do all the senators believe together that could kill them together?**

The Praetorian Guard watches him the whole time. A foreigner's counsel is heard; a foreigner's *orders* are never obeyed. His words inform the Consul — they command nothing.

## The merge

The Consul does not count hands. Where opposing biases agree, that is granite — build on it. Where they collide, the collision itself is the finding: name it, price both sides. And when one lone senator saw something no one else did — sometimes that lone voice is the whole point of convening.

Rome does not average. Rome decides.
